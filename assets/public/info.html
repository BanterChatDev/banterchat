Why Banter exists

A short version of the long story.

Banter started because the chat apps we were already using kept getting worse. Every update added something we didn't ask for — game stores, AI assistants, ID checks, paid features — and quietly broke something we did. Voice would cut out. Notifications would arrive an hour late. Servers would lag during the one moment everyone was actually online together.

So we built the thing we wanted instead. The features people actually use, with the parts that get in the way taken out. Fast voice. Reliable notifications. Servers that load in under a second. Customization that goes deeper than "pick a color."

We kept the parts of Discord that worked — the server/channel/role model, voice channels, slash commands, bots — and threw out the rest. The bloat. The upsell. The constant feature-creep that turns a chat app into something that runs your CPU into the ground.

Banter is run by people who use it every day. When something breaks, we hear about it before the support ticket gets filed. When something feels off, we change it. There's no quarterly roadmap meeting where someone decides what would be good for engagement metrics. There's just: does this make the app better for the people on it, or not.

We don't sell your data. We don't train AI on your messages. We don't have ads in the chat. The only money we make is from people who voluntarily pay for Banter Premium, and that money goes back into running the servers and paying the people who keep them running.

If that sounds like the chat app you've been waiting for, you're in the right place.
